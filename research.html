Here are my research projects sorted by year. Projects tagged as published have been peer-reviewed and published while preprints are published but pending peer-review. I will also include some of the projects in which I'm currently working on — tagged as ongoing.
The Flux Order Relation
Robaina-Estévez & Nikoloski (2019)

We present a new hierarchical organization of Escherichia coli's metabolic network which is based on the flux values that reactions take at steady state. We show that 13C-derived fluxes, transcript and protein levels and enzyme kcat values are in agreement with the hierarchy.
Ordering of metabolite concentrations
Robaina-Estévez (2019)

Experimental observation in E. coli has shown that the concentrations of certain metabolite pairs exhibit an ordering, i.e., one metabolite has always a higher concentration than another, when the system is at steady state. Here, I employ thermodynamic flux balance analysis to derive an ordering of metabolite concentrations in Escherichia coli.
My PhD thesis
Robaina-Estévez (2017)

Cells regulate the activity through metabolic reactions in a context-specific way. The context-specificity of metabolism can be captured by several kinds of experimental data, such as by gene and protein expression or metabolite profiles. This thesis is composed of three individual studies focusing on context-specific experimental data integration into computational models of metabolism aiming at providing context-specific metabolic predictions.
Alternative optima in context-specific metabolic models
Robaina-Estévez & Nikoloski (2017)

Recent methodological developments have aimed at reconstructing context-specific metabolic models from high-throughput data. However, a unique reconstruction is often not guaranteed. Here, we explore this issue and propose possible solutions.
Central metabolism of guard cells
Robaina-Estévez et al. (2017)

Photosynthesis and water use efficiency, key factors affecting plant growth, are directly controlled by the guard cells. Here, we used constraint-based modeling to predict the differences in metabolic fluxes between the mesophyll and guard cells of Arabidopsis thaliana. An independent 13C-labeling experiment provides support for our predictions.
Metabolic network constraints gene expression in Maize
Robaina-Estévez & Nikoloski (2016)

Existing experimental studies have indicated that the emergence of the C4 photosynthesis requires co-ordination between several levels of cellular organization, from gene regulation to metabolism. Here, we utilize flux coupling analysis with maize metabolic models to investigate the correspondence between metabolic network structure and transcriptomic phenotypes along the maize leaf gradient.
Context-specific metabolic model extraction with RegrEx
Robaina-Estévez & Nikoloski (2015)

Context-specific metabolic models aim at describing metabolism under specific scenarios (e.g., cell types). Here, we introduce RegrEx, a fully automated method to reconstruct context-specific metabolic models that relies solely on context-specific data and ℓ1-norm regularization to extract a context-specific model.
Framework for context-specific network extraction methods
Robaina-Estévez & Nikoloski (2014)

Several methods have been proposed to reconstruct context-specific models from existing genome-scale models by integrating various types of high-throughput data. Here we present a mathematical framework that puts all existing methods under one umbrella and provides the means to better understand their functioning, highlight similarities and differences, and to help users in selecting a most suitable method for an application.
