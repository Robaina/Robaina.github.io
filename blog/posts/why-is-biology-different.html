Why is biology different?

1 July, 2019

I have heard some physicists complain about the very nature of biology as a science. It is not a science — they say — it is only about describing reality and not about finding models that generalize and simplify the phenomena we see. Sometimes they even continue — If only physicists were to make biology then it would be a science. But, how accurate is this statement? Let's discuss about it. Full disclaimer: I'm a biologist :).

Physics studies the processes that occur in the universe at a fundamental level. Living organisms are made out of ordinary matter, utterly atoms, utterly(er) made out of only three fundamental particles: top quarks, down quarks and electrons. That's it. Now, physicists have developed a remarkably accurate theory which predicts the whereabouts and movements — rather their probabilities — of these fundamental particles. They have properties: charge, mass, strong nuclear property?, weak nuclear property? and there are forces which act on those properties: electromagnetic force, strong nuclear force, weak nuclear force and gravity.
